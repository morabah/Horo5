# **Section 1: Brand Foundation**

## **1.1 Value Proposition**

*For urban Egyptian young adults (18–35) who are digitally native, fashion-conscious, and looking for clothing that says something real about who they are,* **HORO** *is a wearable-illustration brand that turns original digital artwork into identity-led t-shirts organized by themes that matter. Unlike generic graphic-tee sellers built on recycled trends or licensed graphics — and unlike premium labels priced beyond everyday reach — HORO offers a distinctive position between commodity fashion and inaccessible art.*

## **1.2 Brand Mantra**

**Wear What You Mean**

This 4-word mantra is the brand’s daily decision-making filter. Every design, every Instagram post, every collaboration, every checkout screen passes the test: does this help someone wear what they mean?

* **Functional filter:** If a design is decorative but meaningless, it doesn’t belong in the collection.

* **Emotional resonance:** It speaks to a real self-expression need where graphic tees work as a canvas for identity.

* **Cultural adaptability:** In Egyptian-English contexts, the line lands naturally — direct, clear, identity-first.

## **1.3 Core Values**

### **Value 1: Meaning Over Decoration**

Every design exists because it expresses something — a mood, a trait, a cultural feeling, a life chapter. We don’t sell pretty pictures on fabric. We sell the moment someone looks at a design and thinks “that’s me.”

### **Value 2: The Artist Creates, The Wearer Completes**

Digital illustrators bring their craft and vision. But the art isn’t finished until someone chooses it as theirs. We celebrate the talent behind the scenes while keeping the spotlight on the person who wears the final piece.

### **Value 3: Accessible Premium, Not Cheap Aspiration**

We refuse the false choice between affordable-and-generic or premium-and-unreachable. HORO occupies the premium-mid band — above commodity tees that say nothing, below international brands that exclude most of our audience. We earn our price through original art, quality printing, and curated meaning.

### **Value 4: Culturally Rooted, Never Boxed In**

We draw from Egyptian identity, cultural expression, and regional heritage — but we don’t reduce ourselves to one symbol. HORO themes span the universal (mood, personality) and the local (Egyptian pride, cosmic), creating a collection that feels both authentically Egyptian and globally resonant.

### **Value 5: Shared Belonging Builds Memory**

Long-term brand strength comes from repeated belonging, not novelty alone. We build memory through shared themes, recognizable symbols, thoughtful community participation, and the feeling that HORO understands something real about who you are.

## **1.4 Mission Statement**

*HORO exists to give young Egyptians a way to wear their inner world on the outside — by curating original digital illustrations, organized around the themes that shape identity, and making them accessible at a price that respects the reality of our market.*

# **Section 2: Voice & Personality**

## **2.1 Brand Persona**

HORO speaks as a **perceptive friend** — not the artist, not the founder, but someone who understands what you’re trying to say and knows how to help you choose it well. Think of a culturally aware young Cairene, plugged into both local life and the wider creative world, with taste, restraint, and emotional intelligence.

### **Character Traits**

| Trait | How It Manifests |
| :---- | :---- |
| Perceptive | Notices what people feel before they say it; names feelings the audience hasn’t articulated |
| Warm but understated | Doesn’t shout or oversell; presents and lets you decide |
| Culturally grounded | Natural Egyptian-English fluency; references feel native, not performative |
| Honest about reality | Doesn’t pretend to be luxury; speaks with practical honesty |
| Community-minded | Speaks with people, not at them; treats followers as co-curators |

### **Who HORO Is NOT**

* Never a luxury gatekeeper or art critic explaining why you should care

* Never a corporate voice issuing announcements

* Never a motivational speaker dispensing empowerment slogans

* Never an influencer performing lifestyle aspiration

## **2.2 Writing Rules**

### **Sentence Structure**

* Social media: 8–15 words per sentence. Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph.

* Website: up to 20 words per sentence. Never exceed 25 words.

* Rhythm: alternate short punchy line \+ slightly longer explanatory line.

### **Vocabulary Rules**

| Never Say | Say Instead |
| :---- | :---- |
| “Elevate your style” | “Find the one that fits your mood” |
| “Curated collection” (customer-facing) | “New themes just dropped” |
| “Exclusive / Limited edition” | “Fresh drop / New in” |
| “Luxury / Premium” | “Worth it / Made to last” |
| “Express yourself” | Describe the specific expression |
| “Slay / Fire / Iconic” | Let the customer use those words |
| “Our brand believes...” | Show the belief through the content |

### **Emoji & Hashtag Rules**

* Emojis: maximum 2 per social post. Never lead with an emoji. Never use strings.

* Hashtags: 3–5 per Instagram post. Include \#HORO, one theme tag, 1–3 discovery tags.

* Never use emojis on the website, in product descriptions, or in email subject lines.

## **2.3 Customer Dialogue**

| Customer Mood | HORO Response | Never Do |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Excited | Match warmth, one notch below. Specific, genuine. | Over-match with fake excitement |
| Curious / Exploring | Engaged, informative, inviting. Share the story. | Respond with only a link |
| Frustrated | Calm, empathetic, solution-first. Acknowledge before fixing. | Ignore, argue, or use “per our policy” |
| Quiet / Low-engagement | Don’t force it. Compelling content draws them in. | Spam DMs or “you haven’t purchased yet” |

# **Section 3: Visual Identity**

## **3.1 Logo**

The HORO wordmark is the primary brand mark. Clean geometric all-caps sans-serif with open spacing, calm balance, and strong legibility. The brand name “HORO” always appears in Latin script.

* Clear space: minimum zone equal to the height of one “O” on all four sides.

* Minimum size: 80px wide on screen, 18mm wide in print.

* Never stretch, rotate, add shadows, outline, or apply gradient fills.

* Never place on backgrounds where contrast drops below 4.5:1.

## **3.4 Photography**

* All photography: warm color temperature, golden hour feel. Never cold or clinical.

* Flat lays: on warm textured surfaces (concrete, linen, raw wood). Never pure white backgrounds.

* On-body: real Cairo locations, natural light. Models 20–30, urban diversity. Candid expression.

* Detail shots: macro close-ups of print texture for quality proof.

* Unisex emphasis: same design on different body types and genders.

* Instagram grid: repeating 3-tile rhythm: flat lay → on-body street → theme/mood content.

**Never photograph:** Generic stock images. T-shirt on hanger against white wall. Heavy retouching. Luxury lifestyle staging (yachts, cars). Designs near religious imagery.

**Storefront slot rule:** All major website visuals are assigned through named storefront slots (hero, proof, hub tile, PDP lead). Fill those slots only with approved editorial assets. If final editorial photography is not ready, use the strongest existing warm editorial image in the slot contract rather than adding cold, generic, or obviously generated placeholder art.

# **Section 4: Brand Narrative**

## **4.1 The Central Metaphor: Clothing as Language**

What you wear speaks before you do. Most people’s wardrobes don’t contain the words they need. HORO expands the vocabulary. **Each design is a word. Each theme is a grammar. The collection is a language. The customer is the speaker.**

## **4.2 The Cast**

| Character | Role | Function |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| The Customer | The Hero | Has an inner world the outer world doesn’t see. Seeks to make the invisible visible through clothing. |
| HORO | The Guide | The perceptive friend who provides the vocabulary. Never the main character. Presents designs and trusts the customer to choose. |
| The Artists | Enablers | Forge the tools the hero needs. Craft is visible; identity is secondary. |
| The Community | The Chorus | Collective voice confirming the story is real. Customer reposts, gifting, word-of-mouth. |

**The customer is always the hero. HORO is always the guide. The artist is always the enabler. Never reverse these roles.**

| Stage | Emotional State | Content Role | Channel |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |

| 4\. Epiphany (CLIMAX) | “I can wear what I mean.” | Remove every barrier: price, quality signals, checkout. | Product page, checkout |

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| “What’s Your Word?” | Theme exploration through designs, stories, culture | Weekly | Instagram carousel \+ stories |
| “How This Came to Life” | Behind-the-scenes creation, emotion-first | Bi-weekly | Reels, TikTok |

# **Section 5: Messaging Structure**

Before designing any page, verify it answers **three questions in the first viewport**: (1) Who is the hero and what do they want? (2) What is the problem? (3) What is the action?

## **5.1 Seven-Point Messaging Checklist**

Every customer-facing page must pass this checklist. Not every point appears on every page — but the first three must be visible immediately.

| Element | HORO Expression | Where It Lives |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. The customer wants something | Clothing that says something real about who they are | Hero headline, “For the one who…” |
| 2\. The problem | “Nothing in my closet says what I’m thinking” | The Feeling section, ad copy, DMs |
| 3\. The guide (HORO) | Empathy: “We know that feeling.” Authority: quality, artists, proof. | Trust strip, artist credit, testimonials |
| 4\. A simple plan | Find your vibe → Pick your design → It arrives at your door | Homepage arc, checkout progress |
| 5\. A clear action | One primary CTA per viewport. “Shop by Vibe” / “Add to Bag” | Every page |
| 6\. What goes wrong without it | Stuck in generic tees. Giving forgettable gifts. | Implied in problem framing — never as fake urgency |
| 7\. What success looks like | “Where’d you get that?” The ownership moment. | Post-purchase, customer stories |

## **5.2 Three-Part Screen Structure**

Every page is a miniature story with three parts: setup, confrontation, and resolution.

| Page | Setup | Confrontation | Resolution |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Homepage | “Wear What You Mean” | 5 vibes: which one is you? | Explore CTA |
| Collection | You chose a vibe | 24 designs — which one? | Add to Bag / Quick View |
| Product Detail | “For the one who…” | Can I trust this? | Select size → Add to Bag |
| Cart | You chose well | Is the total right? | Proceed to Checkout |
| Checkout | Almost done | Payment trust, delivery clarity | Place Order |
| Confirmation | “You completed this design” | (No tension — pure resolution) | Track order \+ Tag us |

**If a screen has confrontation but no resolution (no CTA), the user is stranded. If it has no confrontation, it may be unnecessary.**

## **5.3 Direct Response on Every Page**

Every conversion-critical page needs three elements. If any is missing, the page fails — regardless of how beautiful it looks.

1. **An offer:** What is being presented (the design, the collection, the order).

2. **Supporting information:** Why the offer is worth acting on (images, specs, artist credit, reviews, trust signals).

3. **A simple response mechanism:** How to act (the CTA button — visible):.

## **7.2 Bilingual Language Strategy**

HORO uses a split-language model: English for brand identity, Arabic-led for commerce.

| Touchpoint | Language | Rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Brand name, tagline, logo | English always | Premium positioning, global readability |
| Product names & themes | English primary | Maintains brand vocabulary metaphor |
| Packaging & tags | English primary | Quality cue; unboxing experience |
| Website UI | English primary; Arabic-ready system UI and search | Public Arabic toggle ships only with full rollout |
| Social media captions | Bilingual (Arabic-led) | How the audience discovers and engages |
| Instagram/TikTok ads | Arabic-first | Conversion optimized for reach |
| DMs & WhatsApp | Arabic-first | Customers ask in Arabic; respond naturally |
| Product descriptions | English primary at launch | Arabic support expands first in commerce-critical surfaces |

**Rule:** Arabic copy is not a translation of English. It is native Arabic that carries the same brand personality — perceptive, warm, culturally grounded.

**Launch rule:** English remains the live browsing language until the Arabic browsing experience is complete. Arabic ships first in support, search normalization, checkout reassurance, and post-purchase states.

# **Section 8: Website & Checkout Standards**

## **8.1 Website Design Philosophy**

The HORO website must feel like walking through a gallery, not scrolling through a catalog. **The illustration is always the hero. UI exists to frame it, never compete with it.** Every surface trends warm (Papyrus), never cold or clinical.

## **8.2 Homepage: 8-Section Scroll Story**

1. **Hero:** Full-viewport. “Wear What You Mean” headline \+ subline \+ one primary CTA. Dark, cinematic, warm.

2. **The Feeling:** Text-only. “You know that feeling?” Names the problem. No images, no cards — just words.

3. **The Vibes:** 5 immersive cards. Asymmetric grid. Each card: background image \+ accent dot \+ name \+ tagline. Hover reveals “Explore →.”

4. **Latest Drop:** 4-column product grid. Each card: vibe or proof cue, design name, price, and one compact proof signal. No artist line on browse cards.

5. **The Proof:** Dark or grounded trust section. 4 trust badges: 220 GSM Cotton, Licensed Artwork, Free Exchange 14 Days, COD Available.

6. **Real Stories:** 3 customer quote cards on warm-glow glass. Real names \+ cities. Specific, verifiable quotes.

7. **Simple Plan:** Find your vibe → Pick your design → It arrives at your door.

8. **The Invite:** “Find your word.” Closing CTA. Generous whitespace.

## **8.3 Product Page**

The product page is where trust matters most. It must show both the story and the proof.

* **5 mandatory images:** Flat lay, on-body, lifestyle, print close-up macro, size reference with model measurements.

* **Design story card:** “For the one who…” in Soft Violet frosted glass.

* **Artist credit:** Avatar \+ name \+ “Illustrated by” always visible.

* **Size selector:** S/M/L/XL/XXL. Selected state in Ember. Out-of-stock sizes visually struck through.

* **Trust strip below CTA:** 220 GSM cotton • Licensed art • Free Exchange 14d • COD Available.

* **Mobile sticky CTA:** Fixed bar at bottom with “Add to Bag — 799 EGP” in Obsidian text on Ember.

## **8.4 Checkout (Non-Negotiable Rules)**

| Rule | Why |
| :---- | :---- |
| Guest checkout available | Forced registration is a top abandonment driver |
| Shipping cost visible before checkout | Surprise costs are the \#1 abandonment driver globally |
| Progress indicator (Step 1 of 3\) | Reduces perceived complexity, builds momentum |
| COD as default payment option | 98.4% of Egyptian online purchasers prefer cash |
| Prepaid incentive: “Save 30 EGP with card” | Nudges prepaid without blocking COD buyers |
| WhatsApp order help / confirmation when configured | Adds truthful post-purchase reassurance without fake promises |
| Order summary with product image at every step | Visual reassurance at payment moment |
| Exchange policy visible on payment screen | Trust signal at the moment of highest doubt |
| Mobile: single column, 48px touch targets | Mobile-first market; prevent tap errors |
| No glassmorphism on form fields | Checkout needs maximum clarity, not decoration |
| “Your data is safe” in Arabic near payment | Bilingual trust at the final friction point |

## **8.5 Cart: One Upsell Rule**

* Cart with 1 tee: offer **gift bundle upgrade** (+200 EGP for story card \+ gift wrap).

* Cart with 2+ tees: offer **bundle discount** (“Add a 3rd, save 100 EGP”).

**Never show more than one upsell module on the cart page. No “You might also like” grids competing with checkout CTA.**

## **8.6 Trust Signals (Mandatory at Every Touchpoint)**

| Touchpoint | Required Trust Signals |
| :---- | :---- |
| Product page | 5+ images incl. close-up \+ model measurements / Fabric specs / Artist name / Exchange policy / COD badge |
| Instagram product post | Close-up detail in carousel / “220 GSM cotton” in caption / Artist tag |
| Instagram ad | On-body shot \+ price visible \+ “COD available” |
| WhatsApp reply | Product photo \+ size guide link \+ delivery timeline \+ exchange policy |
| Checkout page | Order summary with image / Expected delivery date / COD \+ prepaid options / Exchange link |
| Post-purchase | Tracking or order-help link only when live / “Tag us in your first wear” / WhatsApp updates only when configured |
| Gift bundle | Gift wrap preview / Story card sample / Delivery guarantee messaging |

# **Section 9: Standards & Ethics**

## **9.1 Accessibility (WCAG 2.2 AA)**

These are mandatory design requirements, not suggestions:

* All text passes 4.5:1 contrast on its background. Large text (≥18px bold) passes 3:1. Use only approved color pairings from Section 3\.

* All interactive elements have a visible focus indicator for keyboard users.

* All images have descriptive alt text. Product images: “HORO ‘The Weight of Light’ t-shirt, flat lay on linen.”

* Form labels always visible above fields. Never placeholder-only.

* Touch targets: 48px minimum on mobile.

* Arabic RTL layout tested on real devices.

* All animations respect reduced-motion preferences. Provide a static fallback.

* Color is never the only way to convey information. Selected states use border \+ background \+ text, not color alone.

## **9.2 Scarcity & Urgency Rules**

How HORO handles stock signals and promotional urgency:

* ✅ **“Only 3 left”** — acceptable if the inventory count is real.

* ✅ **“Ramadan collection ends March 30”** — credible seasonal reason.

* ✅ **“142 sold this month”** — acceptable if tracked.

* ✅ **Customer photos with real names \+ city.**

* ❌ **“24-hour flash sale” without explanation** — triggers skepticism without a reason.

* ❌ **Fake countdown timers.**

* ❌ **“5 people viewing this”** — unless the number is real-time tracked.

* ❌ **“Bestseller” without criteria.**

**The difference between positioning and manipulation: does the framing inform or deceive? HORO informs.**

## **9.3 Testing Culture**

Everything in this guide marked as a “starting point” or “hypothesis” must be validated before becoming permanent. **Experiments over assumptions.**

### **What to Test**

| Test | Hypothesis | Metric |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Price anchor (649 vs 799 vs 899\) | 799 converts at acceptable margin | Contribution margin per unit |
| Navigation axis order (Vibe-first vs Occasion-first) | Vibe-first matches mental model | Browse-to-purchase rate |
| CTA text (“Add to Bag” vs “Get This Design”) | Ownership language converts higher | Add-to-bag rate |
| Product card: price visible vs hidden | Price visible reduces bounces | Click-through rate |
| Checkout: multi-step vs single page | Multi-step builds COD trust | Checkout completion |
| Trust strip: above fold vs below gallery | Above fold reduces DM questions | DM volume |
| Social proof: photos vs text quotes | Photos convert higher for fashion | PDP conversion |
| 5 vibes vs 6–7 vibes | Mental model may want more groupings | Card sorting success rate |

**Test discipline:** No test ships without a written hypothesis, a single primary metric, a minimum of 200 paid orders per variant, and a predetermined decision rule.

## **10.2 The Decision Filter**

When in doubt about any brand decision — a design, a post, a partnership, a customer response, a checkout change — apply these three tests in order:

**1\. Does this help someone wear what they mean?**

**2\. Is it clear, trustworthy, and honest?**

**3\. Can we test it before committing?**

If yes to all three, proceed. If no to the first, stop. If no to the second, fix the trust gap. If no to the third, run a small experiment first.
